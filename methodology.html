SPIDER LEAGUE

SDI methodology

Version 0.6 · reviewed June 27, 2026

Definition

Spider Danger Index (SDI) is a 0–10 estimate of the average medical severity experienced by a healthy adult after a confirmed bite from the identified spider taxon.

It excludes encounter frequency, appearance, rarity, and the chance that an observed spider will bite.

Evidence denominator

The preferred denominator is all eligible confirmed bites in a representative study, including bites producing minimal effects. Symptomatic-only series and isolated case reports can describe possible outcomes but do not establish an average.

Cohort-table approximation

Some representative cohorts report the percentage with severe pain and the percentage with systemic effects, but not whether those groups overlap. For those studies, SDI uses 2.5 + 2 × max(severe-pain rate, systemic-effects rate). Using the larger rate once prevents double-counting the same patients. The resulting family or genus estimate is labeled as an approximation and inherited species results are shown with low confidence.

Fallback hierarchy

Species → genus → family → general spider baseline. The app visibly labels the taxon from which the displayed score was inherited.

Safety warnings

An emergency or medical-attention warning is independent of the numerical SDI. This prevents an average-outcome score from concealing a low-frequency but potentially catastrophic outcome.

Pacific Northwest calibration additions

The regional expansion includes a 3.5 genus estimate for Cheiracanthium, a 3.0 low-confidence Agelenidae family estimate, a 3.5 low-confidence species estimate for Araneus diadematus, a 3.0 Araneidae family fallback, a 2.5 Salticidae family fallback, and a 3.0 Steatoda genus fallback. Species-level evidence gaps remain visible even when a higher-level score is displayed.

Additional directly verified taxa

Version 0.6 adds a low-confidence species estimate for Dysdera crocata from eight verified bites and a low-confidence Kukulcania genus estimate from five verified North American bites. Small samples remain visibly labeled.

Current limitations

The evidence database is small and disproportionately supported by Australian prospective bite studies. The general baseline is not a true worldwide average. Ratings should be revised as representative confirmed-bite evidence is added.
